Understanding Sexual Health in Zambia

The Facts About Your Context

🇿🇲
Sexual Health in Zambia

Sexual and reproductive health is a key part of overall wellbeing. In Zambia, young adults face important decisions about their sexual health. Having the right information empowers you to make choices that protect your health and future.

Did You Know?
1 in 4
Young adults aged 15-24 in sub-Saharan Africa account for approximately 1 in 4 new HIV infections, despite being only 10% of the population. (UNAIDS, 2023)

The good news? Most STIs and unintended pregnancies are preventable with the right knowledge and consistent use of protection.

🛡️
Why Condoms Are Essential

Condoms are the only method that provides dual protection — against both STIs and unintended pregnancy.

  • HIV Prevention: Consistently used, reduce transmission risk by ~85–95%
  • STI Prevention: Reduce gonorrhoea, chlamydia, trichomoniasis risk by up to 90%
  • Pregnancy Prevention: 98% effective (perfect use), ~85% (typical use)
  • HPV & Herpes: Reduce transmission risk by 30–70% even for skin-transmitted infections

The difference between perfect and typical use comes down to consistency and correct technique.

📋
How to Use Condoms Correctly — Step by Step
1
Check the Expiry DateNever use expired condoms — latex degrades and they're more likely to break. Check the date on every wrapper before use.
2
Open CarefullyTear along the notched edge using your fingers only. Never use teeth, scissors, or sharp objects — they cause invisible damage.
3
Check OrientationThe rim should be on the outside — the condom should unroll easily. If it's inside out, discard and use a new one (never flip it — pre-ejaculate may be present).
4
Pinch the TipLeave about 1cm at the tip to collect semen. Trapped air causes breakage — always pinch the tip first.
5
Roll Down CompletelyUnroll to the base of the penis. It must cover the entire length. Smooth out any air bubbles as you go.
6
Add Lubrication if NeededWater-based or silicone-based lubricants only. Never use oil-based products (petroleum jelly, coconut oil, lotion) — they degrade latex and cause failure.
7
After EjaculationHold the base of the condom while withdrawing while still erect. Removing too late risks spillage — act promptly.
8
Dispose ResponsiblyWrap in tissue and bin it. Never flush — condoms damage sewage systems and harm the environment.
9
Use a New Condom Every TimeFor every act of vaginal, anal, or oral sex. Single use only — never reuse under any circumstance.
🤰
Unintended Pregnancy — Understanding the Risks
Zambia Statistics (ZDHS, 2018 / WHO)
  • 29% of women aged 15–19 have begun childbearing
  • ~43% of pregnancies in Zambia are unintended

Condoms work by creating a physical barrier preventing sperm from reaching an egg. Common reasons unintended pregnancies occur:

  • Not using any protection — accounts for the majority of cases
  • Inconsistent use — skipping "just this once"
  • Incorrect application or breakage
  • Using expired or damaged condoms
🦠
Common STIs in Zambia

Many STIs have no visible symptoms — this is why protection and testing are non-negotiable.

HIV/AIDS

~11.1% of adults 15–49 in Zambia are living with HIV (MOH, 2023). Highly manageable with ART. Know your status — regular testing is crucial.

Chlamydia & Gonorrhoea

Most common bacterial STIs. Often no symptoms (70% of women, 50% of men with chlamydia show none). Curable with antibiotics when detected early.

HPV

Most common viral STI worldwide. Some strains cause cervical cancer. HPV vaccination available for ages 9–26. Cervical screening important for women.

Syphilis

Bacterial, progresses in stages. Curable early with antibiotics. Increases HIV risk if untreated.

❌ vs ✅
Common Condom Myths vs Facts
Myth
"Condoms reduce pleasure too much to bother."
Fact
Modern condoms are ultra-thin and well-lubricated. Many people report little to no difference. Peace of mind often enhances pleasure. Try different Kiss variants to find what works best for you.
Myth
"If we love each other, we don't need condoms."
Fact
Love doesn't prevent STIs or pregnancy. Many people carry STIs without knowing. Using protection shows love and respect for both partners' health.
Myth
"Two condoms are safer than one."
Fact
Using two condoms increases friction between layers, making both MORE likely to break. Always use exactly one condom.
Myth
"You can tell if someone has an STI by looking."
Fact
Most STIs have no visible symptoms. Someone can look and feel completely healthy while carrying an infection. This is why protection and testing are both essential.
Myth
"No need for a condom if she's on the pill."
Fact
The pill prevents pregnancy but offers zero protection against STIs or HIV. Use dual protection for comprehensive coverage — condom plus hormonal contraception.
⚔️
Why Dual Protection Matters

Dual protection means combining a condom with another contraceptive method such as the pill, injectable, implant, or IUD.

Why it's recommended:

  • Hormonal contraceptives are highly effective against pregnancy but offer zero protection against STIs
  • Condoms alone have a typical-use failure rate of ~15% for pregnancy
  • Combining both brings protection to over 99%
  • Especially recommended for young people, those with multiple partners, or those not recently tested

Kiss Condoms + your preferred contraceptive = maximum coverage.

🚨
Emergency Contraception & PEP

Emergency Contraception (EC):

  • Levonorgestrel pills (like Lydia Postpil): Most effective within 72 hours, works up to 120 hours
  • Effectiveness decreases with time — take as soon as possible
  • Available at pharmacies, clinics, health centers
  • Does NOT protect against STIs
  • Not intended for regular use — less effective than regular contraception

HIV Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP):

  • Must start within 72 hours of potential exposure (sooner is better)
  • 28-day course of antiretroviral medication
  • Seek medical help immediately

⚠️ EC and PEP are backup options, not replacements for regular protection.

Consent and Healthy Relationships

Sexual health isn't just about physical protection — it's about respect, communication, and consent.

What is Consent?

  • ✦ Freely given — no pressure or coercion
  • ✦ Enthusiastic — both genuinely want to
  • ✦ Informed — understanding what you agree to
  • ✦ Specific — yes to one ≠ yes to everything
  • ✦ Reversible — anyone can change their mind

🟢 Green Flags

  • Open communication about protection
  • Mutual respect for boundaries
  • Willingness to use condoms
  • Support for regular STI testing
  • Feeling safe to say no or stop

🔴 Red Flags

  • Pressure to go without protection
  • Anger when you want condoms
  • Refusal to get tested
  • Any form of coercion
  • Making you feel bad for saying no
You have the right to insist on protection. A partner who refuses condoms or pressures you into unprotected sex is showing a serious red flag. Your health and safety are non-negotiable.

Getting Tested: Know Your Status

Regular testing is a normal, responsible part of sexual health care. It's not about lack of trust — it's about taking care of yourself and your partners.

When to Get Tested

  • ✔ Before becoming sexually active with a new partner
  • ✔ At least once a year if sexually active
  • ✔ Every 3–6 months with multiple partners
  • ✔ Immediately if you think you've been exposed
  • ✔ After any unprotected sex

Where to Get Tested in Zambia

  • ✔ Government health centres (free or low-cost)
  • ✔ Provincial and district hospitals
  • ✔ PPAZ clinics (Planned Parenthood Zambia)
  • ✔ Marie Stopes Zambia clinics
  • ✔ Mobile testing units in communities
  • ✔ University health services

What to Expect

  • ✔ Quick, simple process
  • ✔ HIV rapid tests: results in 15–20 minutes
  • ✔ Most tests involve urine or blood samples
  • ✔ All testing is confidential
  • ✔ Healthcare workers are non-judgmental

Counselling Guide
GATHER Counselling Framework
  • Greet — respectful, non-judgemental
  • Ask about needs, history, current methods
  • Tell about relevant contraceptive options
  • Help client make an informed choice
  • Explain how to use chosen method
  • Return — schedule follow-up visit
Dual Protection
Dual Protection Recommendations

WHO-recommended combinations:

  • Condom + Combined Oral Contraceptive
  • Condom + DMPA (Depo-Provera)
  • Condom + Subdermal Implant
  • Condom + IUD (copper or hormonal)

Especially important for adolescents, high-prevalence settings, and clients with multiple partners.

Breakage Protocol
Condom Breakage — Clinical Response
  • Assess timing relative to ovulation; offer EC within 72–120hrs
  • Conduct HIV risk assessment — offer PEP within 72hrs if indicated
  • Educate on correct condom technique
  • STI testing: 2 weeks (bacterial), 6–12 weeks (HIV)
  • Schedule follow-up counselling appointment
Sensitivity
Latex Allergy Considerations
  • Latex allergy: ~1–6% of general population
  • Symptoms: localised itching, urticaria, rarely anaphylaxis
  • Alternatives: polyurethane or polyisoprene condoms
  • Female condoms (nitrile-based) are latex-free
  • Spermicide sensitivity may mimic latex allergy
Youth
Youth-Friendly Counselling
  • Non-judgemental, confidential communication
  • Acknowledge peer pressure; normalise safe sex discussion
  • Clarify: carrying a condom is responsible, not promiscuous
  • Encourage both partners in contraception discussion
  • Practical demonstration with anatomical model where available
Extra Time
Kiss Extra Time — Clinical Notes
  • Contains benzocaine (mild topical anaesthetic) — interior surface only
  • Minimal transfer to partner in typical use
  • Contraindicated: known sensitivity to local anaesthetics
  • Barrier efficacy fully preserved — meets WHO/ISO standards
  • Advise client on symptoms of sensitivity

Frequently Asked Questions

Got Questions?

Clear, honest answers about Kiss Condoms and sexual health — no judgment, just facts.

Are condoms 100% effective?
+
No contraceptive method is 100% effective, including condoms. When used correctly and consistently, male latex condoms are approximately 97–98% effective at preventing pregnancy (perfect use). In real-world conditions (typical use), effectiveness is around 85%, accounting for human error.

For maximum protection, combine condom use with a hormonal contraceptive method (dual protection). Condoms remain the only method that simultaneously reduces risk of both pregnancy and STIs including HIV.
Can condoms expire?
+
Yes. Every condom has an expiry date printed on the individual wrapper and the box. Latex degrades over time — expired condoms are weaker, more prone to tearing, and significantly less effective.

Always check the expiry date before use. Stored properly (cool, dry place, away from sunlight and sharp objects), condoms last up to 5 years from manufacture. Condoms kept in wallets or exposed to heat may degrade faster, even before their stated expiry.
What does 53mm mean?
+
53mm refers to the nominal width of the condom — the width when the condom is laid flat. All Kiss Condom variants are 53mm nominal width, which is the international standard and fits the majority of men comfortably.

When unrolled, a 53mm condom accommodates a girth of approximately 100–106mm (~10–10.6cm). Latex is highly elastic and stretches considerably, so 53mm is a universal starting size that fits most people well.
What if a condom breaks?
+
1. Stay calm. A single incident doesn't guarantee pregnancy or STI transmission — but it does increase risk.

2. Emergency Contraception. If pregnancy risk exists and it's within 72 hours, take EC (sooner = more effective). Some options work up to 120 hours. Visit a pharmacy or clinic immediately.

3. HIV PEP. If HIV exposure risk exists, visit a clinic or hospital within 72 hours for Post-Exposure Prophylaxis.

4. Get tested. Arrange STI testing at 2 weeks (chlamydia/gonorrhoea) and 4–6 weeks (HIV).

5. Review your technique. Common causes: not leaving space at the tip, oil-based lubricants, opening with sharp objects, or using an expired condom.

For stockist information or bulk orders, contact DKT: +260 97 7725114, +260 97 9351203, or +260 97 5620011. Follow @KissCondomsZM on social media for availability updates.
Can I use oil-based lubricants with Kiss Condoms?
+
No. Oil-based products (petroleum jelly, baby oil, coconut oil, lotion) degrade latex and can cause condoms to break — significantly reducing protection.

Always use water-based lubricants with Kiss Condoms. Silicone-based lubricants are also compatible with latex condoms.
What is Kiss Extra Time's delay lubricant?
+
Kiss Extra Time contains a mild topical anaesthetic called benzocaine applied to the interior of the condom. It reduces sensation for the wearer to extend duration, while the exterior standard lubricant ensures comfort for both partners.

Transfer to a partner is minimal in typical use. If either partner experiences numbness, itching, or discomfort, discontinue and consult a pharmacist. Avoid this variant if either partner has a known sensitivity to local anaesthetics.
Do condoms protect against all STIs?
+
Condoms significantly reduce risk of most STIs, with effectiveness varying by type:

Highly effective against: HIV, gonorrhoea, chlamydia, hepatitis B, trichomoniasis — infections spread primarily through bodily fluids.

Moderate protection against: Syphilis, genital herpes (HSV-2), HPV — these can spread via skin-to-skin contact in areas not covered by the condom.

Consistent condom use combined with regular STI testing provides the strongest overall protection. HPV and hepatitis B vaccines are also available — speak with a healthcare provider.
What is Kiss Strawberry — is it safe?
+
Kiss Strawberry is a premium latex condom with a strawberry scent and coloured latex. It provides the same level of protection as Kiss Classic — the scent and colour do not reduce effectiveness.

It is suitable for both oral and penetrative sexual activity. If you or your partner are sensitive to fragrance additives, consider using Kiss Classic instead. Always check for skin reactions and consult a pharmacist if uncertain.
